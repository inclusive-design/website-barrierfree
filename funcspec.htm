Introduction

The purpose of this document is to specify the desired functionality of the various deliverables of the CANARIE project Creating Barrier-Free Broadband Learning Environments. This document will serve as a reference for the technical team implementing the systems.

There are two 'classes' of intended users for this project: learners, who are primarily users of the Player deliverable, and educators, who are primarily users of the Authoring Tool deliverable.

This document is presented in two sections. The first section describes the intended users of the systems, indicating the assumptions made regarding their abilities, and discussing the user characteristics which drive the functional specifications. The second section presents these specifications.

Target Learners

Primary

The primary learners being targeted by this project are learners who are:

  • Blind and low vision
  • Deaf and hard of hearing
  • Physical Disabilities
  • Learning Disabilities

The systems will be designed to directly address the needs of these groups.

Secondary

The secondary learners being targeted by this project are as follows:

  • English as a second language
  • Learners without disabilities
  • Deaf-blind

The systems will not attempt to directly address the needs of these groups, but effort will be made to avoid introducing new barriers for these groups.

Reading Level for Learners

It is assumed that the target learners will have a minimum Grade 4 reading level.

Content Type

The content which will be used for evaluation of this project will be as follows:

  • Physics: first 2 clusters of "World in Motion" series, 10 videos initially
  • Humanities: the best 3 of "Room for Five"

Primary Barriers and Requirements for Learners

The current primary barriers for learners are as follows:

For deaf/hard of hearing learners:

  • lack of captioned content
  • lack of readable captions
  • identification of speaker
  • understanding language used
  • understanding vocabulary used
  • understanding concepts used
  • speed of content/captions

For blind/low vision learners:

  • Math notation, Science notation, Geography maps, accounting charts

Text to speech products have great difficulty with graphics environments (diagrams, charts, maps, etc.). When reading charts, it is sometimes difficult to read columns without a previous knowledge of how they should look.  Also, alignment is most important with charts, and this has to be made clear to persons who are unable to see what the charts really look like.

Goals for Learners

The tasks that we want the learners to achieve with our curriculum and with our tools are as follows:

  • Success understanding the content
  • Greater academic success
  • Easy use of tool
  • Obtain support for using the tools
  • For blind/low vision learners: obtain adequate descriptions of visual material

Target Educators

There are two types of target educators: the original content creators, and 'modifiers' who refine the content created by the content creators for their own purposes. The first group would include content developers and web designers. The second group would include teachers, students and captioners.

Technical Proficiency of Educators

It is assumed that the target educators will be:

  • Web browsing literate
  • Have word processing skills
  • Have an awareness of students' needs
  • Be technically savvy, and motivated

Primary Barriers and Requirements for Educators

The current primary barriers for educators are as follows:

  • lack of support for creating content for multiple types and levels of learners
  • lack of support for making "readable" captions
  • lack of support for transcribing and insertion of captions
  • lack of support for importing line 21 captioned content
  • lack of support for different skill levels of creators

Goals for Educators

The tasks that we want the educators to achieve with our tools are as follows:

  • create enhanced interactive content that includes hyperlinking captions
  • address the varying needs of learners
  • ease of use of authoring tools


Functional Requirements

This section describes the desired functionality from the user perspective (learner or educator, as appropriate), in terms of both the content itself, and the interface with the content. The desired functionality of the Player drives the functional requirements of the Authoring Tool.

1 Content

This project involves enhancing digitized videos with various forms of learning objects including text captions, audio descriptions, and more. Several types of information will be presented to the learner:
 

Main video
In most cases, this is the original video-based learning material.
Captions
A verbatim text caption will be the base alternative format for the learning material. Alternatives to the verbatim caption may also be available, such as reduced reading level captions or alternate languages. For more information, see the section on Captions.
Audio descriptions
Audio descriptions of the visual elements of the video will also be available. Like the text captions, different types of audio descriptions will be available, such as didactic and descriptive. For more information, see the section on Audio Descriptions.
Sign translation
A sign translation video image will be available. This may possibly consist of multiple images, if necessary to convey multiple speakers, etc.
Markers
Graphical overlays may be used by the author to draw the learner's attention to particular areas of the visual image or text.
Graphics
In addition to graphical overlays for highlighting purposes, other graphical images may be presented if deemed appropriate by the content author.
Annotations
The author/educator may add text annotations to the material to provide a form of commentary on the lecture. For learners with visual impairments, these annotations can be spoken aloud.
Other
Other, as yet defined information may be added as well.

Some of these media types are described in more detail below.

1.1 Captions

Captions provide a non-auditory alternative to the audio portion of a video. Learners will be able to choose from alternative versions of text captions. Possibilities include (but are not limited to) the following:

  1. Verbatim caption
  2. Speed-Reduced caption
  3. Modified Reading Level caption
  4. Multilingual versions of the above types of captions
  5. Other

In some cases, multiple versions of a type of caption may be used. For example, if the original audio is in English, there will be only one verbatim caption, in English, but there may be several multilingual captions, each in a different language. Similarly, several different reading level captions may be used. See the section entitled Learner Controls for information on learner selection of captions.

Text captions will be able to include graphical images, and hyperlinks to other material (see the section entitled Hyperlinks for more details).

The presentation of text captions will be defined by the content author using the Authoring Tool, and will to some extent be configurable by the learner using the Player. The presentation features which will be configurable are:

  • Background
    • Shading
    • Transparency/opaque and colour options
    • No background
  • Background shape
    • Standard rectangle edit box
    • Text background wraps to text on each line (TV broadcast pop-up captions)
    • Speech bubble and other shapes
  • Font face (all available fonts including non-latin fonts)
  • Font size, style, colour

Text captions will appear in one of several preset positions in the Player window:

  1. Outside of the video frame of the movie container window.  For instance, in the black border below, above or to the side of the video frame.
  2. Inside of the video frame of the movie container window
  3. Overlapping the video frame of the movie container window
  4. In an HTML-based text field outside of the movie container window
  5. In an HTML-based scrollable caption storage field outside of the movie container window.  This window accumulates all the captions for the video.  The caption storage field contents may be selected and copied to clipboard.  The contents of this field may also be saved as a file.

More information on learner control of the positioning and formatting of captions is presented in the section entitled Learner Controls.

1.2 Audio Descriptions

Audio descriptions provide non-visual alternatives to the visual content of the video. The following types of audio descriptions will be predefined:

Standard
This description will contain a typical audio description of the visual elements, designed to fit into the quiet times of the video.
Expanded
This description will contain a typical audio description of the visual elements, but will not be limited in length to the available quiet time (see further explanation below).
Multilingual versions of the above types of descriptions
These descriptions would contain, to the extent possible, the same content as those described above, but in alternative languages.

Two further classifications of audio description will also be possible:

Didactic
This description will contain the strictly educational content of the visual scene, that is, a description of only (and all) that which is necessary to understand the intended educational lesson.
Extra
This description will contain any extra description, not contained in the didactic description, which might be appropriate, such as descriptions of the scene, the appearance of the characters, etc.

The expanded version of the audio description will not be edited to fit into the quiet time available in the original audio.  Presentation of this content will be carried out in one of two ways, based on the learner's preference:

  • the original material will automatically be paused while the audio description plays, and playback will resume automatically once the audio description is complete; or
  • the learner will be informed of the presence of additional audio description by an audio tone, and may opt to pause the original playback to listen to the audio description if they desire.

1.3 Annotations

Annotations are very similar to text captions, except that they will typically be composed by the teacher, and serve to provide supplementary information, as opposed to alternative formats of the primary information. Characteristics such as font features and screen positioning are the same as those for captions.

1.4 Hyperlinks

Any text in a text caption or annotation, or any graphical image, can be a hyperlink. Unlike traditional web-based hyperlinks, caption hyperlinks will have up to four types of information associated with them:

  1. Definition
    2. a text definition of the word or phrase
  2. Interactive exercise
    3. an interactive exercise designed to illustrate a concept or idea
  3. Background information
    4. text presentation of related information which may be helpful in understanding the word or phrase
  4. Related link(s)
    5. hypertext link(s) to web page(s) containing material related to the word or phrase

A hyperlink may also be used to take the learner to another location in the presentation.

Information about the presentation and activation of hyperlinks is provided in the Player section on hyperlinks.

2 Player

The Player is the interface through which the learner interacts with the educational material.

2.1 Layout

The educational material contains several types of media, from video to text to graphics. The Player will control the layout of this information on the screen, and process the learner's interaction with the material.

Through the Player, the learner will be able to select which of the available forms of content they wish to view, as well as the format used to present this information (see Learner Controls for more information). As well, other information will be presented in the Player window if the learner chooses. These include:

  • the full caption text, presented in a separate area of the screen,
  • a full list of all links contained in the caption,
  • the author's annotations,
  • the learner's own notes (see Learner Notes for more information on learner notes),
  • indication of where in the lecture the learner is currently (e.g. title/section etc),
  • navigational controls

2.2 Navigation

The learner will have the ability to navigate the video presentation. Specifically, learners will be able to:

  1. start, stop and pause playback;
  2. warp immediately to the beginning or end of the video;
  3. move to different, arbitrary positions in the video;
  4. increase the playback rate to advance the video more quickly;
  5. move directly to meaningful locations within the video, such as beginning of chapters, sections;
  6. search for words in captions and move from section to section containing that word; and
  7. activate links for access to the associated information

2.3 Hyperlinks

When a learner activates a hyperlink, the playback of the educational material will pause, and the learner will be presented with a list of the available information associated with that link (as described above), and will be able to select from that list.

A list of all of the links in a presentation will be available to the learner at all times, in a separate area of the display. The learner will be able to navigate directly to the location in the material where a link appears using this list.

For learnerw with visual impairments, an audio indication will be played when links are presented on screen. Learners will be able to pause the playback, and hear a list of the links available in the current phrase of the caption, presented using text-to-speech (TTS). Activation of the link will cause the associated information to be read using TTS.

2.4 Learner-Created Captions

Learner has access to limited authoring tool and can make custom caption text tracks to share with other learners. The tool will not allow for more elaborate kinds of caption creation such as variable positioning over the video. The caption track is saved as a file which can be added to the Learning Objects Repository.

2.5 Learner Notes

Learners will be able to 'take notes' while viewing a video. These notes will be kept locally (i.e. not uploaded to the Learning Objects Repository), and will be available on future playbacks of the same material. Notes differ from Learner-Created Captions in that they are intended for personal use, not for presentation to other learners, and are not overlaid on top of the video as captions are.

Learners will create notes by pausing the video at the desired time, entering the desired note (for example, by typing), and saving the note. Learners may enter text into notes, and may mark text with a hyperlink to other material. Learners may not embed images or other non-text elements directly into notes.

The note will be time stamped at the specified location for synchronization in later playbacks.

The learner will be able to specify whether or not notes are displayed in the Player window. If so, the notes will be presented in a text list, and learners will be able to navigate to the video portion associated with any given note by selecting the note from the list. During playback of a video that has notes, an indication (visual and/or auditory) will be made when/where notes have been added. Learners will be able to select whether they would like visual cues, auditory cues, or both.

Learners will also be able to use the player to view their own notes without viewing the associated learning materials.

2.6 Alternative Outputs

The Player will support devices such as refreshable braille, text to speech, haptic devices, as well as output in various accessible medium. It will be compatible with current adaptive technology text/screen readers (Jaws), Screen enlargers, etc. or will implement an equivalent functionality.

2.7 Learner Preferences

To the extent permitted by the author (see explanation), the learner will have the ability to select what information presented, and how it is presented. Specifically:
  1. Learners will be able to specify which alternative formats (captions and/or audio descriptions) they wish to be presented. They may select to display more than one text caption simultaneously. For example, a student learning English as a second language may wish to display both the English captions and the captions in their own language. The learner may select alternative audio captions with reduced background sound for greater understanding.
  2. Learners will be able to define the appearance of the text captions. For example, a learner with low vision may prefer a very large font size, or a particular colour combination.
  3. Learners may control the placement of text captions, as described above.
  4. Learners will be able to adjust the positioning of the video images (main video and sign language interpretation)

Learners will be able to specify their preferences for configurable features using a Learner Preferences System. These preferences will be used whenever the learner uses the Player to view content.

The learner will be able to modify a subset of the attributes 'on the fly', using either on-screen widgets or keystrokes. The attributes which will be modifiable this way include:

  1. Turning captions on or off
  2. Choose condensed versus full captions
  3. Positioning video images and text captions
  4. Setting the audio volume

These changes will apply only to the current session.

3 Authoring Tool

The authoring tool will allow authors to classify captions according to their functions (e.g. language, reading level). The author will be able to define new custom caption types if so desired.

Authors will be able to individually configure the attributes of text-based captions, including font face, size, colour and background colour, and container type.

For each visual track (including text-based captions and graphics) authors will be able to specify the position, on screen, of the caption. The authoring tool may add a blank area around the original video that could be used for placement of caption items which are desired to be NOT overlaid on top of the video. Even if the author specifies a default position which is overlaid, this border will remain, so that the user may override the positioning and place the caption outside the video.

Because authors may wish to use the position or formatting of captions to convey information, the ability of the learner to specify their own preferences for these attributes creates the risk that such information may be lost. For this reason, the author will be able to specify whether or not the specified formatting of captions may be overridden by the user. Three levels will be used:

  1. Cannot be overridden at all
  2. May be overridden, but user will be warned that this action may result in loss of information
  3. May be overridden without warning
