Significant Digits

 

The smallest unit, of the scale used, limits the precision of any measurement. The smallest division of the ruler on the right is one millimetre. Any measurement made with this ruler can only be reported to the nearest 0.1 mm. This requires the last digit in the measurement to be an estimate. Thus the length of the pencil can be reported as 134.5 mm or 13.45 cm, the first three digits are certain the last digit is an estimate.

Significant digits are used to communicate the precision of measured values. Measurements are reported using all the numbers you can read directly from the scale of the instrument you use and one uncertain, or estimated, digit.

Counting significant digits:

  • digits 1 - 9 are considered significant
  • zeros between any digits 1 - 9 are significant; trailing zeros are considered ambiguous, in this series all trailing zeros are significant
  • leading zeros are not significant

Manipulation of Data

The rules of significant digits used throughout this series are those commonly adhered to in scientific fields and studies.

  1. When only adding or subtracting, the calculated answer should be rounded to the same degree of precision as the least precise value used in the computation, that is the value with the fewest number of decimal places.
  2. When multiplying or dividing, the calculated answer should be rounded to the same number of significant digits as the value with the fewest significant digits.
  3. When a series of calculations is performed, all calculations should be carried out with the calculator running, and the final answer rounded to the same number of significant digits as the quantity with the fewest significant digits.

In this series apparent inconsistencies may arise from time to time when a series of calculations is performed. When intermediate results are written, or printed in graphics, they are rounded to one more significant digit than required for the final result. However, this result is not carried forward, and you should not use it when verifying calculations.
