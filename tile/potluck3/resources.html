SCIENCE AS A WAY OF KNOWING: Understanding the Fur Trade in Canada

Learning Objective

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to locate relevant information about the impact of the fur trade on Canada's Native people, using a variety of online resources.

Resources:

Suggested Learning Outcomes:

Learn about geography
Learn about the Fur Trade in Canada
Learn about scientific knowledge and what is termed “local knowledge”
Learn how scientific knowledge intersects with local knowledges
Learn how to apply local knowledges to scientific knowledge and vice versa
Learn about cultural differences
Learn about the history of the Idaa trail
Learn how to find and evaluate information on the Web

Key Terms:

Scientific truth; Consensus; Standardization; Equivalences; Technical devices; Social strategies; Technosciences; Localness

Objectives:

1. Distinguish between science and technology.
2. Explain the role of technology in the pursuit of science.
3. Discuss the linkage(s) between science, technology and society.
4. Apply knowledge acquired in the Turnbull’s article to explain factors that contribute to the destructive nature of science.
5. Link effects of social strategies and technical devices that science has developed to the ‘stories’ ‘told’ by the displays at the Ontario Science Centre (OSC).
6. Outline the limitations of science.
7. Discuss the ‘real’ power of science.
8. Explain, using examples, how local knowledges, other than ‘school science’, make sense of the material world.

Study Guide:

1. What characteristics does science share with other local knowledges?
2. Why is the notion of ‘local’ important in ‘thinking’ about knowledge?
3. What makes science such a powerful discipline?
4. Why is it important to learn how different cultures explain and interpret natural phenomena?

Learning Activities:

Learn about the Idaa trail and about Native peoples’ reactions to the Fur Trade;
Learn how to locate and evaluate relevant online information, and add this to a list of links for the class to share.
