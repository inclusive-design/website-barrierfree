Work and Power

 

Whether using an escalator or an elevator to move from one floor to the next the result is the same. Both mechanical devices move a person by doing work against gravity from the first to the second floor. Since gravity acts vertically down, to overcome it a vertical force must be applied up.

To determine the amount work done against gravity we multiply the magnitude of the applied force by the distance through which it acts. In the case of the elevator, it is easy to see that the distance between the floors is to be used.

Because the escalator moves the person along an incline there is the question of which distance to use, along the incline or the vertical distance. The key is that both distance and force must be in the same direction. Since the applied force overcoming gravity is vertically up, the effective distance must also be vertical and up.

Therefore, in both cases the vertical distance is used to calculate the work done.

The escalator does more work in moving Devendra to the second floor because his mass, 55 kg, is greater than Laura's at 45 kg is. The escalator does 1.73*103 J of work compared to the elevator's 1.41*103 J, a significant difference.

However, when we consider power, the rate of doing work, this example makes an interesting point. Laura, using the elevator reaches the second floor in 13 s, whereas it takes Devendra 25 s, almost twice as long. Using:

Power = work ÷ time

We can determine the power developed by both, the elevator and the escalator.

Pelevator = Welevator ÷ televator
Pelevator = 1.41 × 103 J ÷ 13 s
Pelevator = 108 J/s
Pelevator = 1.1 × 102 W

Pescalator = Wescalator ÷ tescalator
Pescalator = 1.73 × 103 J ÷ 25 s
Pescalator = 69.2 J/s
Pescalator = 69 W

Clearly the elevator developed more power by doing less work much faster. Power depends on how fast work is done.
